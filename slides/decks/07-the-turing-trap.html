The Novella — a passage

She covers her mouth, which is a reflex she has — covering the laugh, containing it, because somewhere in the architecture of her childhood she learned that loud feelings are dangerous — and then takes her hand away because no one is watching. She does this thing where she recalibrates mid-sentence, as if the first version of a thought is never the one she trusts, and her eyes — which are the kind of eyes that have been told they're beautiful so many times the compliment no longer carries information — do a quick scan of the room that isn't checking for danger but for witnesses. She is alone. She laughs again. The sound is different the second time. Less curated.
