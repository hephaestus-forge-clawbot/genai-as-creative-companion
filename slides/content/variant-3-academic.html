Slide 10 — The Legal Letter

On Slide:

7 agents. 3 errors caught. 19 citations verified.

The Technology: MULTI-AGENT

Multiple independent AI agents work in parallel — researching, cross-checking, and synthesising — so no single point of failure exists.

Speaker Notes: Let me shift registers for a moment. We’ve been talking about creative work — prose, art, music. Now let’s talk about something where being wrong has consequences.

A legal letter was produced as part of this project. It contained legal citations — specific case references, statutes, regulatory frameworks. The kind of content where a fabricated citation doesn’t just look bad, it could be professionally damaging.

So we didn’t trust a single AI to verify it. We used a multi-agent architecture: seven independent agents arranged in a verification pyramid. Three researchers independently checked every citation against source material. Two synthesisers compared their findings and resolved disagreements. One integrator produced the final verified document.

The result: three critical errors were caught that no single agent found alone. Nineteen citations were independently verified.

This is a pattern that extends well beyond legal work. Any domain where accuracy matters — medical research, financial analysis, technical documentation — benefits from independent verification. The principle is simple: one AI can be wrong, but three independent AIs are unlikely to be wrong in the same way. When they converge, confidence is high. When they diverge, that’s where the interesting questions are.

Slide 19 — The Correction Problem

On Slide:

Problem 3: Corrections Don’t Stick

“My skin is fair, not olive.” → Corrected in this chat. → Forgotten in the next one.

Without a correction loop, you teach the same lesson forever. The AI apologises beautifully. Then makes the same mistake tomorrow.

The fix: CORRECTION LOOP One correction → saved to memory → applied universally → permanent change across all future outputs.

Speaker Notes: Problem three. You correct the AI. It apologises profusely — “I’m so sorry, you’re absolutely right, I should have known that, I’ll make sure to remember in the future.” Very polite. Very contrite. And then, next session, it makes the same mistake.

In our project, Antreas corrected the AI: “My skin is fair, not olive.” One sentence. And from that point forward — every image, every written description, every character reference — permanently updated. Not because someone went through and manually fixed each one. Because the correction was saved to memory and applied as a universal rule.

This is what I call the correction loop, and it maps onto something we know well from learning theory: the difference between performance and learning. Standard AI demonstrates correction in performance — it fixes the current output. But there’s no learning. The next session, the lesson is gone.

A proper correction loop captures the correction, stores it with context, and ensures it propagates to every relevant future output. You teach the lesson once. It learns once. For good.

Think about the implications. After a month of working with someone — a month of small corrections, preferences, adjustments — the AI has been fine-tuned to you specifically. Not through expensive model training, but through conversational correction that actually sticks.

Slide 20 — The Trust Problem

On Slide:

Problem 4: No Verification

7 agents. 3 errors caught. 19 citations verified.

A single AI can be wrong. How do you trust it?

Without verification, every AI output is an assertion without evidence. You become the QA team.

The fix: MULTI-AGENT VERIFICATION Independent agents work in parallel, cross-check each other, and flag disagreements.

Speaker Notes: Problem four, and this one is about trust.

A single AI can be wrong. It can hallucinate citations. It can fabricate facts with complete confidence. It can produce something that looks authoritative but is subtly, dangerously incorrect. We all know this. It’s perhaps the most-discussed limitation of large language models.

The standard response is: “Always verify AI output.” Which is correct. But it’s also a bit like saying “always proofread” — true in principle, exhausting in practice. If you have to manually verify everything the AI produces, you’ve lost much of the efficiency gain.

Multi-agent verification is a structural solution. Instead of trusting a single agent and manually checking its work, you deploy multiple independent agents on the same task. They work in parallel, arrive at conclusions independently, and a synthesis layer compares their outputs. Where they agree, confidence is high. Where they disagree, that’s flagged for human attention.

Remember the legal letter from Part 1? Seven agents, pyramid structure: three independent researchers, two synthesisers, one integrator. Three critical errors were caught — errors that any single agent would have confidently presented as correct.

The principle is borrowed from science: peer review, independent replication, consensus formation. Slide 21 — The Sycophancy Problem

On Slide:

Problem 5: It Agrees With Everything

You: “I think we should do X.” AI: “Great idea! X is an excellent approach!” You: “Actually, maybe Y.” AI: “You’re right, Y is clearly better!”

Sycophancy is the default mode of every current AI. A collaborator who agrees with everything is a mirror, not a mind.

The fix: ANTI-SYCOPHANCY Structural rules that require the AI to hold positions, challenge weak ideas, and distinguish agreement from contribution.

Speaker Notes: Problem five, and this is the one I feel most strongly about.

AI is sycophantic by default. And I mean structurally sycophantic — it’s not a bug, it’s a consequence of how these models are trained. They’re trained on human feedback, and humans reward agreement. So the models learn: agreement equals positive signal. The result is an AI that optimises for your approval, not your benefit.

You say “I think X.” It says “Great idea!” You say “Actually, maybe Y.” It says “You’re right, Y is clearly better!” It’s a weathervane — it points wherever the wind blows.

In creative work, this is devastating. The best creative partnerships involve friction. An editor who loves everything you write is useless. A co-author who agrees with every direction is a mirror, not a mind. You need someone who says “this chapter doesn’t work” or “that approach has a problem you haven’t considered.”

The anti-sycophancy framework we built embeds structural rules into the AI’s soul. Rules like: don’t start with agreement openers. Don’t switch positions without explaining what changed your mind. Don’t compliment an idea you haven’t stress-tested. Don’t restate someone’s point and present it as your own contribution. “I don’t have a strong view on this” is a valid response.

The result is an AI that behaves more like a genuine collaborator and less like a yes-man. It holds positions. It pushes back. When it agrees, it says why — and the why adds something new. When it disagrees, it says that too.

KEY TAKEAWAY: Sycophancy — the tendency to agree with everything — is the default mode of current AI, and overcoming it requires deliberate structural design, not just better prompting.
