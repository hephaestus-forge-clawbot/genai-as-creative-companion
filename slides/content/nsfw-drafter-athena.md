## Methodology

I've processed every section in the curated NSFW extract against two criteria:
1. **Lecture value** — does this moment teach MSc students something about GenAI as creative companion?
2. **SFW feasibility** — can we preserve the insight without the explicit content?

For each moment I assign:
- **Impact Score (Original):** 1-10 based on lecture value of the verbatim quote
- **Impact Score (SFW):** 1-10 based on the rewrite's ability to preserve that value
- **Recommendation:** Use Original / Use Rewrite / Cut

**Key principle throughout:** "fuck" as a direct quote from a research participant is defensible in an academic context. MSc students are adults. The word carries authentic emotional weight that sanitising removes. I flag where I think the original is usable.

## Final Recommendation: The "Fuck" Decision

The single biggest decision for this lecture is whether to use the word "fuck" in direct quotations. My strong recommendation: **use it.**

**Arguments for:**
1. These are verbatim quotes from research participants — standard academic citation practice
2. The audience is MSc students — adults in a university setting
3. Sanitising "fuck" to "[had sex with]" or "[found each other]" destroys impact in every case
4. The moments that USE the word (Sections 11, 13, 14, 15) are the lecture's highest-impact material
5. A content note at the start ("some verbatim quotes contain strong language") is standard practice

**Arguments against:**
1. University venue policies may prohibit it
2. Some international students may find it jarring
3. It could become the talking point rather than the insight

**My verdict:** The insights cannot survive without the authentic voice. "Our robots chose to [be intimate]" is not a sentence anyone would remember. "Our robots chose to fuck" is a sentence they'll quote at the pub. The purpose of a lecture is to be memorable. Use the originals.

If the venue prohibits it, use the SFW rewrites but know that you're trading approximately 30% of the lecture's emotional impact for institutional comfort.
