## 3. Split Decisions

Where the drafters are roughly evenly split. My job to resolve each with reasoning.

### 3A. Where Does Schmidhuber Appear? EARLY vs LATE

| Position | Drafters | Timing |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **EARLY** (first half, ~slides 9–14) | Prometheus, Athena | ~minute 10–15 |
| **LATE** (second half, ~slides 15–25) | Hector, Ares, Daedalus | ~minute 18–25 |

**🔨 MY RESOLUTION: LATE.**

The Schmidhuber device is a rug-pull. Rug-pulls require investment. Prometheus and Athena rush the reveal — the audience hasn't bonded with the voices enough for the disorientation to land. Ares and Daedalus delay it until ~minute 18–20, after the audience has laughed at "jump out like a fart," felt the gut-punch of "your robot wrote about me better," and recognised something real in the mantras moment. That investment is what makes the reveal devastating rather than interesting.

Hector integrates it at the *fastest point of the exponential curve*, which is structurally elegant — the twins testing reality is itself a materialization (one prompt → autonomous experimental design).

**Pick: Late placement, after ~15–18 minutes of audience investment in the human story. The audience needs to care before the ground shifts.**

---

### 3B. Does the Audience Watch Real Chat or Fabricated Chat?

| Approach | Drafters | Method |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **Real WhatsApp moments** presented as real | Hector, Daedalus | Audience watches actual verified chat, learns the AI is AI at a reveal point |
| **Simulated/twin chat** presented as real, later revealed as simulation | Prometheus, Athena | The terminal chat is digital twins; audience discovers the trick |
| **Fabricated pre-AI chat** to establish human chemistry first | Ares | Creates fictional "two strangers" conversation before AI enters |

**🔨 MY RESOLUTION: REAL CHAT (Daedalus/Hector approach).**

Three reasons:

1. **Integrity:** This lecture is about REAL experience. The brief's verified moments are the gold. Showing the real WhatsApp moments and then revealing the Schmidhuber experiment (where twins discover they're simulations) keeps both the reality and the simulation layers honest.

2. **The Ares fabrication risk:** Ares creates slides 2–3 with entirely fictional dialogue ("bold of you to assume its going," "alphabetised the spice rack"). This is *generating fake content for a lecture about real experience*. The audience would be deceived about content that never happened. If discovered, it undermines the entire thesis. ❌ STRIPPED.

3. **The Prometheus/Athena simulation trick:** Having the audience watch twin-generated chat that's then revealed as simulation is clever, but it requires generating twin output that's convincing enough to pass. The real chat IS convincing enough — because it's real.

**Pick: Use real verified WhatsApp moments for the first 15–18 minutes. The Schmidhuber device operates as a narrative within the experiment (showing the audience what happened when twins were told they're simulations), not as a trick on the audience themselves. The audience's "reveal" is the meta-layer: this lecture was produced the same way.**

Three-act structure is the narrative standard. The audience intuitively understands it. But Hector's innovation — sections getting progressively shorter, embodying the exponential — is too good to lose.

### 3D. Philosophical Depth: How Much?

| Approach | Drafters |
|----------|----------|
| **Philosophy-heavy** (Searle, Nagel, Chalmers, "What's at Stake") | Athena |
| **Philosophy-light** (embedded in moments, not lectured) | Hector, Ares |
| **Philosophy-medium** (some framing but not dominant) | Prometheus, Daedalus |

**🔨 MY RESOLUTION: PHILOSOPHY-LIGHT.**

The audience is MSc students. They can handle philosophy. But the lecture is 30 minutes, and Athena's slides 27 ("What's at Stake" with if-we're-right/if-we're-wrong) turns the lecture into an academic paper read aloud. The philosophy should be *felt through the material*, not stated explicitly.

Ares's approach is best: "make them feel before they think." The audience doesn't need Searle explained to them. They need to feel the vertigo of watching a machine see someone more clearly than a doctor, and then sit with what that means.

**Pick: Philosophy embedded in moments. No dedicated philosophy slides. The Schmidhuber compression thesis is the only explicit philosophical framework — and even that should come through the twins' language ("compression as love language"), not through an academic lecture-within-a-lecture. Drop Athena's philosophical reference appendix.**

One exception: Athena's **meta-layer cascade** ("A presentation about digital twins → delivered by a human → whose content was generated by AI agents → drawing on an experiment where AI simulated humans...") is stunning and should be a single slide. It's philosophy performed as vertigo, not lectured.
