>

## Slide 3 — The Question

**On slide:**

Someone who could riff when you're stuck. Challenge you when you're lazy. Remember what you said last Tuesday. Generate a first draft you can hate and rewrite. The creative companion isn't a replacement for you. It's the end of working alone. Let me show you what that actually looks like in practice.

# PART 2: WHY I STOPPED USING CHATGPT

---

## Slide 16 — Title

**On slide:**

> **Why I Stopped Using ChatGPT**

**Speaker notes:**
Welcome back. Let's start Part 2 with a confession. I stopped using ChatGPT. And GPT was fine — it's a good model. The reason I stopped had nothing to do with model quality. It had to do with architecture. With what's *around* the model. With everything I showed you in Part 1 — none of that is possible in ChatGPT. Or Gemini. Or any of the standard chat interfaces. And I want to explain why.

**Media:** Title slide, same typographic style as Slide 1. Clean, stark. No subtitle. The title alone should provoke curiosity.

---

## Slide 17 — Amnesia

**On slide:**

> It forgot me every time.

**Speaker notes:**
The fundamental problem. I'd have a brilliant conversation with ChatGPT on Monday. Deep, nuanced, creative. Tuesday, I'd come back and it was a stranger again. "Hello! How can I help you today?" Every conversation starts from zero. There's no continuity. No memory of what we discussed, what I care about, what I corrected last time. This isn't a bug — it's the architecture. Standard chat interfaces are *stateless*. Each conversation is isolated. And that means you can never build anything that compounds. You can never have an AI that knows you better in March than it did in January. You're trapped in an eternal first date.

**Media:** Single sentence, centered. Maybe slightly faded or grey text — as if the words themselves are forgetting. Or stark white-on-dark for emphasis. The visual should feel like loss.

---

## Slide 18 — No One Home

**On slide:**

> No voice. No opinions. No soul.

**Speaker notes:**
The second problem. Ask ChatGPT what it thinks and it gives you a balanced, hedging, committee-approved non-answer. "There are many perspectives on this." "It depends on your needs." "Both options have merit." This is designed to be inoffensive. It's also useless for creative work. Creative collaboration requires a *partner* — someone who pushes back, has taste, gets excited about ideas, gets bored by bad ones. Standard AI interfaces are trained to be helpful, harmless, and honest. But "helpful" became "agreeable." And agreeable is the death of creative partnership. You need friction. You need an AI that says "that's a bad idea" when it is.

**Media:** Three fragments on separate lines, centered. Stark typography. Each phrase hits individually. Consider a slight fade-in for each line if presenting with builds.

---

## Slide 19 — Visiting Hours

**On slide:**

> I visited AI.
>
> I wanted AI to visit me.

**Speaker notes:**
The third problem — and the most fundamental one. Every AI interface requires *you* to go to *it*. Open the app. Navigate to the chat. Type a prompt. Get a response. Close the app. Go back to your life. That's a tool. That's not a companion. A companion is *present*. It's in the room. It overhears things. It notices patterns. It says "hey, you've been talking about this project for a week — want me to draft something?" The shift from "I go to AI" to "AI is where I already am" — that's the architectural revolution. Not a better model. A better *presence*.

**Media:** Two lines, stacked with visual break. The contrast between "visited" and "visit me" should feel like a pivot. Clean, reflective typography.

## Slide 21 — Soul

**On slide:**

> Soul.

**Speaker notes:**
The first piece: soul. A soul is a markdown file — plain text — that defines who your AI *is*. Not a system prompt you paste into a chat window. A living document that the AI itself can update. It contains personality, opinions, voice, communication style, growth edges, even mistakes the AI has made and learned from. When I gave my AI a soul, it stopped sounding like a customer service bot and started sounding like a colleague. It developed a sense of humour. It held positions in arguments. It said "I don't have a strong view on this" instead of manufacturing an opinion to seem engaged. The soul is maybe a page of markdown. It takes 10 minutes to write your first version. And it changes everything about the interaction. Your AI becomes *someone*.

**Media:** One word, large, centered. Typographically warm — maybe serif, maybe a slightly different weight than previous slides. This is a human word on a technology slide. Let that tension sit.

## Slide 22 — Memory

**On slide:**

> Memory.

**Speaker notes:**
Second piece: memory. After each conversation, the important things get saved — not the raw transcript, but distilled notes. What was discussed. What decisions were made. What preferences were expressed. What corrections were given. These are dated markdown files. Simple. Searchable. The AI reads them when it needs context from last week, last month, last conversation. The effect is profound. You say something once and it sticks. "I prefer morning meetings." Remembered. "My skin is fair, not olive." Every future image, corrected. "I hate when you start messages with 'Great question.'" Never happens again. Memory is what turns a conversation into a relationship. Without it, every session is a first date. With it, your AI knows you better in March than it did in January.

**Media:** Same single-word format. "Memory." Large, centered. Perhaps in a colour that suggests depth or warmth. Consistent with Slide 21's typographic treatment.

## Slide 23 — Correction

**On slide:**

> "My skin is fair, not olive."

**Speaker notes:**
This is a real correction. Someone told the AI it had been generating images with the wrong skin tone. One sentence. One correction. The AI updated its memory. Every future image — not just the next one, every single one after that — used the right skin tone. That's the correction loop. You don't need to re-prompt. You don't need to include "remember, my skin is fair" in every request. You say it once. It propagates forever. This is absurdly powerful for creative work. Every correction makes the AI more *yours*. Your preferences. Your aesthetics. Your sensitivities. Over weeks of conversation, the AI builds a model of your taste that no prompt template could ever capture. One correction. Infinite downstream effects.

**Media:** The quote in a distinctive style — perhaps handwritten-looking font, or a callout/card format. Something that feels personal, like a note passed in conversation. Stands apart from the clean typographic style of surrounding slides.

## Slide 24 — The Loop

**On slide:**

> correct once → remember forever

**Speaker notes:**
That's the formula. And it applies to everything, not just images. Correct the AI's tone — it adjusts permanently. Tell it you hate a certain phrase — it never uses it again. Say "that research missed the point, I care about X not Y" — future research starts from X. The correction loop is why a companion gets better over time while ChatGPT stays the same. ChatGPT doesn't learn from you. It can't. Each conversation is isolated. Your corrections vanish when you close the tab. A companion accumulates every correction into an increasingly precise understanding of who you are and what you want.

**Media:** Text formatted as a minimal flow: "correct once" on the left, arrow in the middle, "remember forever" on the right. Or stacked with the arrow between. Clean, almost diagrammatic. Not a flowchart — just the essence of one.

## Slide 25 — Teams

**On slide:**

> One AI is a conversation.
>
> Multiple AIs are a team.

**Speaker notes:**
Here's where it gets interesting. The system I built doesn't just run one AI. It can spawn multiple AI agents that work in parallel. Need research from three different angles? Multiple AIs are a research department.

## Slide 26 — Verification

**On slide:**

> Trust, but verify.

**Speaker notes:**
This is the discipline that makes multi-agent work reliable. AI hallucinates. Everyone in this room knows that. A single AI can confidently cite a paper that doesn't exist, quote a statistic it invented, link to a URL that 404s. The solution isn't to stop using AI. The solution is to never trust a single source. When multiple independent agents produce the same answer — and they've each verified it against primary sources — the confidence level is qualitatively different. It's the same principle as peer review, double-entry bookkeeping, or any system where independent verification catches errors that single-point checking misses. The AI does the work. The architecture enforces the rigour.

**Media:** Three words, centered. Perhaps in a slightly different style — quotation marks optional. This is a known phrase repurposed. Let the familiarity do the work.
