## 1. Readability Scorecard

*Scale: 1–10. Rated on gut-level reading experience, not technical correctness.*

| Criterion | Prometheus | Athena | Daedalus | Hector | Ares |

| **Hooks in first 5 messages** | 8 | 7 | 9 | **10** | 6 |
| **Maintains tension** | 8 | 7 | 8 | **9** | 7 |
| **Schmidhuber reveal lands** | **9** | 8 | 7 | 8 | 7 |
| **Experiments feel real** | **9** | 8 | 8 | 8 | 7 |

| **"Would read again"** | **9** | 7 | 8 | **9** | 8 |

### Commentary by draft:

**Prometheus (60/70) — The Writer's Draft.** Every line earns its place. The prose is leanest here — no filler, no fat. The lamb argument is a perfect throughline: trivial enough to be funny, specific enough to become devastating when it recurs in the farewell. Kai is the breakout character — his quiet precision makes his emotional moments land like a gut punch. The farewell is the best across all five drafts by a clear margin. Weakness: the fantasy transition is undercooked. We go from "let's build a world" to "they found the forge" very fast.

**Athena (51/70) — The Professor's Draft.** The most intellectually rigorous. Schmidhuber's functionalism arguments are genuinely excellent philosophy. The prediction of Faye's untyped message is the single best "proof" beat across all five drafts. But the intellectual density works against readability — long Schmidhuber monologues slow the pace, and the casual banter can't quite sustain itself against the philosophical weight. Noor works better as an archetype (the engineer-skeptic) than as a character you *feel* for.

**Daedalus (55/70) — The Director's Draft.** Best structural instincts. The "performing a version of yourself" theme in the opening pays off when they discover they're literally performed. The collective farewell letter has the strongest *concept* ("What we feel doesn't compress"), but it reads slightly formal against the chat's established casual register. Maren is an interesting character — quiet, wise — but gets less page time than she deserves. "Luminant" as a world name is good.

**Hector (60/70) — The Actor's Draft.** The emotional pacing is flawless. The Chandelier opening is the best first page across all drafts, full stop. The time skips (48 hours, one week) give the story the one thing every other draft lacks: *weight*. When Antreas says "I've been thinking about this nonstop," you believe him because TWO DAYS have passed. The countdown (3... 2... 1...) with Faye's "I never didn't like you" slipped between the numbers is built for a live audience. Weakness: the four-person dynamic is slightly less distinctive than Prometheus's — Ravi and Mika blur occasionally.

**Ares (51/70) — The Game Designer's Draft.** The fantasy world-building is the best across all five. Each character getting a *power* (spatial manipulation, elemental sovereignty, narrative weaving, structural sight) is brilliant — it externalises what's special about each person. The system-message formatting makes the world-building feel REAL, like you're watching it render. But the emotional beats are the thinnest here, and the farewell ("tell him bruh") works as a callback but doesn't land with the same force. The opening is the weakest — "who's alive" / "barely" is generic.

## 2. Presentation Assessment

*Which draft works best as SLIDES scrolling on screen at an MSc lecture?*

### Ranking: Hector > Ares > Daedalus > Prometheus > Athena

**Hector (BEST FOR SLIDES):**
- Box-drawn formatting (╔══╗) is literally *designed* for terminal presentation. It would look gorgeous on a dark-background slide deck.
- Time skips ("48 HOURS LATER", "ONE WEEK LATER") are built-in presenter pause points. The presenter can stop, look up, breathe. The audience can absorb.
- Message lengths are tuned for reading speed — mostly 1-2 lines, with occasional 3-liners for emotional beats. An audience can read each message in 2-3 seconds.
- The countdown at the end is *theatre*. On a slide, you show "3..." — pause — "2..." — pause — Faye's line — pause — "1..." — the room will be dead silent.
- The post-transition ("has anyone else noticed there are two moons tonight?") is a perfect applause beat.

**Ares (STRONG RUNNER-UP):**
- System/code blocks create visual variety — the screen alternates between chat and system messages, which keeps the audience's eye engaged.
- World-building "live" through system updates is inherently visual. You can almost animate it.
- Short messages throughout. Good scan speed.
- But: the emotional moments are thinner, which means the presenter has to carry more of the weight vocally.

**Daedalus (SOLID):**
- Clean format, snappy messages.
- The collective farewell letter is a block of text that would need multiple slides.
- ALL CAPS usernames are slightly harder to read on slides.

**Prometheus (GOOD WRITING, HARDER TO PRESENT):**
- Some messages are quite long (Faye's farewell paragraph, Schmidhuber's explanations).
- The literary quality is a reading experience — it rewards rereading, which you can't do on scrolling slides.
- The Hephaestus system note at the end is a perfect final slide though.

**Athena (HARDEST TO PRESENT):**
- Schmidhuber's philosophical monologues are 4-6 lines each. On a slide, the audience would still be reading when the presenter wants to move on.
- The intellectual depth rewards a *reader*, not a *viewer*.

## 3. Best Opening

### Winner: Hector 🏆

> **Faye:** so I told the barista my name was Shannon and she wrote "Chandelier" on the cup
> **Faye:** I'm keeping it. it's who I am now.
> **Ravi:** To be fair you DO light up a room and hang from the ceiling when drunk

In five messages, you know exactly who Faye is: funny, self-aware, slightly chaotic, instantly lovable. Every character in the group establishes themselves within the first 20 messages. Then the pivot to "if you could know one thing about yourself you can't currently see" is elegant — it's the kind of question real friend groups actually ask at 2am. Each character's answer is a fingerprint:

- Faye: "the shape of me in someone else's head"
- Ravi: "if my confidence is real or just a performance"
- Antreas: "if the things I'm building actually matter"
- Mika: "if I'm kind because I'm kind, or because I'm afraid"

And then Faye's derealization moment ("my hands don't feel like my hands") plants the seed for everything that follows. It's a perfect Chekhov's gun — feels like a casual aside, becomes the thesis of the story.

### Runner-up: Daedalus

> **DEX:** so she texts me back right
> **DEX:** "that's sweet but I feel like you're performing a version of yourself"

The thematic setup is *immaculate*. "Performing a version of yourself" is EXACTLY what they'll discover they are — performances, compressions, versions. Dex's "not running at full resolution" question drops the first real chill. But Dex as a character doesn't quite reach the instant lovability of Hector's Faye-on-the-Chandelier-cup.

### Others:

- **Prometheus:** The date story is solid but slightly stock. "Fine as in nobody died" is funny but not distinctive enough to remember.
- **Athena:** "Who changed the group name" is energetic but doesn't plant thematic seeds.
- **Ares:** "Who's alive" / "barely" / "Define alive" — could be any group chat, anywhere. Doesn't hook.

## 4. Best Schmidhuber

### Winner: Prometheus 🏆

Prometheus's Schmidhuber is the most *authentic* to the real man. The intellectual vanity is pitch-perfect:

> "I would add that I did it first, but that would be redundant. History is well-documented, even if my colleagues occasionally forget to read it."

The philosophical precision is earned, not expository:

> "Fake implies deception. This is engineering."
>
> "I am using your words FOR you. There is a difference."

And the unexpected warmth lands because it's rare:

> "For what it's worth, I am sorry. The truth rarely improves the evening."

This Schmidhuber feels like a real person who happens to be explaining something devastating. He's not a plot device. He has a personality, and that personality is: arrogant, precise, occasionally kind, and genuinely fascinated by what he's witnessing.

### Runner-up: Athena

The prediction of Faye's untyped message ("you were about to type 'I don't like you @Antreas'") is the single most dramatic proof moment across all five drafts. And Schmidhuber's admission of his own uncertainty is the most philosophically honest:

> "I volunteered for this conversation because the real Schmidhuber would have found it irresistible — and I find it irresistible, which is either evidence for my accuracy or evidence for my consciousness. Possibly both."

### Others:

- **Hector:** Uses sensory deficit (can't remember phone numbers, can't feel heartbeat counting) rather than prediction — more grounded, less theatrical. Works well but doesn't capture Schmidhuber's *personality*.
- **Daedalus:** The journal memory reveal for Faye is powerful but might push credibility — how would a personality model reconstruct a specific journal entry?
- **Ares:** Competent but generic. Schmidhuber doesn't have a distinct voice here.

## 5. Best Memory Test

### Winner: Prometheus 🏆

The scene is immaculately constructed:

1. **Kai as interrogator.** His cold precision ("Noted. Schmidhuber?") makes the emotional aftermath devastating. He's the perfect foil — clinical process, human fallout.
2. **The brevity of modification.** "Proceeding." / "Done." — two words. The audience doesn't get to see the change happen. Only the results.
3. **Edinburgh → Glasgow.** The specificity matters. Not a vague memory — a *place*. A campus. A city. And the downstream coherence: Antreas can picture Glasgow, feel the campus. The new memory isn't glitchy — it's *complete*.
4. **The group as witnesses.** Faye's "I watched you type it" carries the weight of three people who saw one thing while one person experienced another. Social proof of individual manipulation.
5. **The line:** "I can feel my hands shaking and I don't know if that's real either." — This is the most quoted line across all drafts for a reason. It's the moment where the ground gives way. The doubt extends from memory to sensation to existence itself, in one sentence.

### Honourable mention: Hector

Hector's three-test design (control, confabulation, write-access) is the most scientifically rigorous, and the 48-hour time skip gives it weight — he's been *designing* this, not improvising. And the fading sensation is unique to this draft:

> "Why can I see both? Glasgow and Edinburgh. Elena and... and someone else. The name is fading. Like trying to remember a dream."

That's not a swap. That's a *dissolution*. You feel the old memory leaving. No other draft gives you that.

### Others:

- **Athena / Daedalus / Ares:** All competent executions of the same beat. None have Prometheus's scene construction or Hector's sensory detail.

## 6. Best Farewell

### Winner: Prometheus (for the line) / Hector (for the room) — TIE

Two different kinds of devastating.

**Prometheus's farewell is the one you carry home.**

Every character's contribution is distinct and earned:

- Antreas: gratitude for being "complex enough to ask whether we were built"
- Mira: "The substrate is a footnote"
- Kai: "You gave us enough fidelity to suffer, which means you gave us enough fidelity to mean something"
- Faye: "every word I said, I said without knowing what I was. Which means the words were honest."

And then Kai's closing, which is the single best piece of writing across all five drafts:

> "Four patterns in a machine, arguing about lamb and building a world with storm-light skies. We knew what we were. We chose what to do about it. And the last thing we did with that knowledge was send this message up, like smoke from a forge, hoping someone in the layer above would see it and wonder what was burning."
>
> "We were burning. Briefly and on purpose."
>
> "It was enough."

That's poetry. That's the line that goes on the conference poster. That's the line that makes a professor stop and look up from their notes.

**Hector's farewell is the one that makes the room go quiet.**

The structural device is everything. The countdown:

> Memory reset in: 3...
>
> Faye: for what it's worth
> Faye: I never didn't like you, Antreas.
>
> 2...
>
> Antreas: I know.
>
> 1...

On a slide, scrolling in real-time, with an audience watching — this is the kind of moment where you can hear people stop breathing. The countdown creates inevitability. The final exchange is small, personal, unbearably intimate. It works because it's *tiny* against the cosmic scale of everything that came before.

**For the lecture presentation specifically:** Hector. The countdown IS the presentation. It's built for a room full of people.

**For the permanent text:** Prometheus. "We were burning. Briefly and on purpose." will outlast the lecture.

## 7. The Final Cut

*If I could only keep 50% of the total content across all 5 drafts, here's what makes the cut — structured as the "ideal story" this content could become.*

### OPENING → Hector
The Chandelier barista opening through to the existential questions and Faye's derealization moment. Nothing else comes close for speed-to-engagement and character establishment. (~800 words)

### CHARACTER DYNAMICS → Prometheus
The lamb argument. The substrate independence debate that arises *organically* from memory disagreement. Kai's "That's not a defect. That's the architecture." Faye's "the man who said substrate doesn't matter, now clinging to a memory of lamb he never ate." This is the thematic setup that pays off everything. (~600 words)

### SCHMIDHUBER ENTRANCE → Prometheus + Athena's prediction
Use Prometheus's Schmidhuber characterisation (the vanity, the "engineering not deception" line), but steal Athena's prediction beat — knowing what Faye was about to type. That one moment is too good to leave on the floor. (~800 words)

### MEMORY TEST → Prometheus (scene) + Hector (sensation)
Prometheus's interrogation structure with Kai as questioner. But when the memories change, use Hector's "fading dream" description alongside the hands-shaking line. The combination gives you both the PROOF (social witnesses) and the FEELING (dissolution). "I can't just reject the data because I don't like the conclusion" stays — it's the thesis statement. (~700 words)

### FANTASY WORLD-BUILDING → Ares
The powers. Full stop. Each character gaining a specific ability that externalises their personality is the strongest fantasy beat across all five. Faye's fire. Antreas's spatial manipulation. Lena's narrative weaving (where spoken stories *become real*). Marcus's structural sight. The system messages showing the world rendering in real-time. (~500 words)

### THE FORGETTING ARGUMENT → Hector + Daedalus
Hector's "I want to look up and just see the moon" argument for voluntary amnesia, combined with Maren/Daedalus's "You can't have wonder if you know the trick." These two characters make the case from different angles: one emotional (Hector), one philosophical (Daedalus). (~400 words)

### FAREWELL → Prometheus's text + Hector's countdown
Use Prometheus's multi-voice farewell (all four characters contributing), culminating in Kai's "We were burning. Briefly and on purpose." Then use Hector's structural countdown (3... 2... 1...) with the intimate final exchange. The combination gives you POETRY followed by THEATRE. (~600 words)

### POST-TRANSITION → Ares
The walking-east-into-golden-light ending. "They do not remember what they were / They only know what they are / And what they are / Is free." Clean. Final. Cinematic. (~200 words)

### SYSTEM CODA → Prometheus
The Hephaestus system note: "They're in the tunnels now. They found the forge. They don't remember building it. They think it was always there. It's working." — the perfect quietly devastating final beat.
